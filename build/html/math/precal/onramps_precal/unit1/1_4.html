1.4 Sequences and Differences¶

1.4.1 Sequences¶

What are Sequences?¶

Definition:

A pattern of numbers in a particular order

Example:

10, 20, 40, 80, 160, ...

The First number is the first term and so on.

Formula:

\(f(n) = an^k\) or \(f(x) = ax^k\)

Where \(n\) is a Natural Number (also represented as \(x\)), \(a\) is a Real Number, and \(k\) is a Positive Integer.

Finite (Triangular) Differences¶

Key Points:

The level of the Constant Difference is the same as the Exponent (\(k\))

The Constant Difference can be found by Calculating the Factorial of the Exponent (\(k!\))

Note

Finite Differences are sometimes referred to as Triangular Differences

Deriving Formula from Sequences¶

When given a sequence, break it down into smaller sequences of differences between the terms (do this until the differences are equal, this is the Common Difference used to get the Constant Difference).

The number of smaller sequences you need to make until you find that number is known as the Level of Difference (this is the \(k\) value in the formula).

To calculate the Constant Difference set up an equation in the following format:

\(k! \times a = x\)

Where \(k\) is the Level of Difference (ie. the Exponent), \(x\) is the Common Difference, and \(a\) is the Constant Difference. Solve for \(a\).

Example:

5   8   11  14  17
  3   3   3   3     <- 1st Difference

1st Difference = 3

Example:

1   4   9  16  25  36
  3   5   7   9  11    <- 1st Difference
    2   2   2   2      <- 2nd Difference

2nd Difference = 2

Example:

1   8   27  64  125  216   343
  7   19  37  61   91   127     <- 1st Difference
    12  18  24  30   36         <- 2nd Difference
      6   6   6    6            <- 3rd Difference

3rd Difference = 6

Sequence Formula Patters¶

Below is a table of Sequence Formulas that will help you identify other Sequences.

Type of Function

Formula

Examples

Linear

\(f(x) = ax^1\)

\(3x^1\) when 1st Difference = 3

Quadratic

\(f(x) = ax^2\)

\(1x^2\) when 2nd Difference = 2

\(2x^2\) when 2nd Difference = 4

\(3x^2\) when 2nd Difference = 6

Cubic

\(f(x) = ax^3\)

\(1x^3\) when 3rd Difference = 6

\(2x^3\) when 3rd Difference = 12

\(3x^3\) when 3rd Difference = 18

Note

Notice how the Levels of Difference are the same as the Exponents in the Expressions
